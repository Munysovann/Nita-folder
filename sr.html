Overview of Angkor Wat

Angkor Wat is a world-renowned temple complex in Cambodia, originally constructed in the early 12th century by King Suryavarman II. It is the largest religious monument in the world and a symbol of Cambodia's cultural heritage. Initially dedicated to the Hindu god Vishnu, it was later transformed into a Buddhist temple. Angkor Wat is famous for its grandeur, intricate carvings, and impressive scale.

History of Angkor Wat

Angkor Wat was built during the height of the Khmer Empire and was dedicated to the Hindu god Vishnu, reflecting the king's devotion. The construction took several decades to complete and involved thousands of artisans, laborers, and engineers. After the fall of the Khmer Empire, Angkor Wat became a Buddhist site, which it remains today. The temple is now a UNESCO World Heritage site and continues to be an important pilgrimage destination for Buddhists worldwide.

Architecture of Angkor Wat

Angkor Wat’s architecture is a stunning example of classical Khmer architecture. The temple is built with sandstone and has a distinct layout, featuring five towers that represent the peaks of Mount Meru, the center of the Hindu universe. The temple complex is surrounded by a large moat, symbolizing the cosmic ocean. The intricate bas-reliefs on the walls depict scenes from Hindu mythology, including the famous Churning of the Ocean of Milk.

Visiting Angkor Wat

Angkor Wat is located in Siem Reap, Cambodia, and attracts millions of tourists annually. Visitors can explore the temple complex, walk through its grand corridors, and marvel at the exquisite carvings. Sunrise and sunset views at Angkor Wat are particularly popular among tourists, offering breathtaking views of the temple against the changing sky. It’s recommended to arrive early to avoid crowds and to fully appreciate the temple’s serene atmosphere.
